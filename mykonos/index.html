Greece

Mykonos

studied

The history of Mykonos before the Revolution does not show us any brilliant pages. It is the history of a place that only barely, and with difficulty, managed to survive through the centuries, at least until the twentieth century.

Aristidis Romanos, architect

Indeed, the most interesting events and characteristics of its historical development are precisely those that describe the often agonizing effort of its inhabitants to devise a way of surviving on a rocky place which, beyond the usual products of the self-sufficient economy of the Cycladic islands, such as wine and livestock products, always had as its principal resources abundant sun, air, sea, and also neighboring Delos. To some extent, the island’s periods of greatest prosperity were those that offered the best possibilities for exploiting these natural resources. This ecological reading is what gives the history of Mykonos its particular interest and instructive value; a brief overview of it follows.

Ancient settlement

Very little is known about the settlement of the island during the ancient historical period. The most likely possibility is that there were two towns on the island, according to Scylax, who lived in the time of Darius, and certainly more than one. One of them appears on Ptolemy’s map as fortified. The most probable sites of the towns are:

  • Palaiokastro, on a hill east of the bay of Panormos, where in the medieval period a fortified mansion of the Ghizi family was built, and where today the monastery of the same name stands.
  • The Kastro of Chora, which is one of the sites with clear traces of prehistoric Cycladic culture and is advantageously located in relation to the bays of Agios Stefanos and Tourlos and of Korfos and Ornos.
  • The site of Lino, where ruins survive of two towers from the Classical period, probably watchtowers, as well as sections of wall.

It seems, however, that beyond the towns, “the Mykonians, who in ancient times appear to have been almost all farmers, preserved, like the Aeginetans, the tendency to live in scattered dwellings in their fields.” This settlement form has survived until today.

Medieval decline and Ottoman rule

During the medieval and modern periods, demographic fluctuations and socio-economic developments formed broader regional phenomena that determined the fate of Mykonos and the other Aegean islands. These developments led them from the misery of the end of Frankish rule to the economic prosperity of the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries.

The main stages of this process are the following:

In the last decades of the sixteenth century and during the seventeenth century, the situation of Greek subjects was tragic. The internal decline of the Ottoman Empire, the weakening of central authority, the penetration of European powers into the trade of the East, and, at the same time, wars such as the Venetian and Turkish wars, conversions to Islam, enslavements, massacres, mass migrations to Italy, and internal migrations to mountainous and island regions, all significantly reduced the Greek population.

The Greek population was reduced from about 1,500,000 to 700,000 by 1700, and certain places were periodically abandoned. Mykonos in the middle of the sixteenth century was almost deserted; between 1633 and 1667 it was almost uninhabited. Around 1678 only a small number of houses are mentioned, while in the years 1674, 1687, and 1692 the island is recorded as having about 2,000 inhabitants.

The Koinon of the Mykonians

A historical moment of particular importance was 1615, when the Koinon of the Mykonians was established, that is, when the local centre of self-government, which had already been allowed in 1537 by Kapudan Pasha through the granting of privileges, became institutionalized. The memoranda and petitions of the Koinon to the Pasha, together with the notarial acts of the period, constitute valuable sources of information not only about living conditions on Mykonos in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, but especially about the poverty and crushing debts of the community to the island’s creditors.

On 8 October 1615, the people and clergy of Mykonos gathered outside the Kastro at the church of Prophet Elias, overthrew the government of the captains, and founded the Koinon of the Mykonians. By universal vote, held every year on 1 March, the islanders elected trustees and other officials, and ratified the election through a notarial act between the community and the trustees. The Koinon sent embassies to Constantinople and elsewhere, judged common disputes, imposed fines and penalties, dealt with taxation and customs duties, and managed other economic privileges.

In the eighteenth century, the political unity of the Greek world was restored under the Turks, and the great period of economic prosperity of the “enslaved” Greeks began. With European commercial penetration into the Balkans, and with the establishment of houses and representatives in Turkish ports, Greeks first acquired the role of local brokers and intermediaries. Later, however, after accumulating their own capital, they covered the entire commercial circuit themselves, establishing shipping enterprises and building their own vessels in local shipyards, and even in foreign shipyards.

Chora, or Mykonos, is the only settlement of some size. The rest of the named places on the island, Ano Mera, Platis Gialos, Ornos, and others, are only denser concentrations of scattered dwellings.

Economy: agriculture, the sea, and piracy

On Mykonos, economic life had long been based on agricultural and livestock production and on maritime activities. Agricultural and livestock production was limited. The only significant export product was wine, with which the Mykonians supplied the Venetian army in the Morea. The main driving force of the island’s economy, however, was the maritime professions: shipping, always in combination with trade, fishing, boat-building and boat repair, and even piracy and the slave trade.

There are indications that Greeks participated in piracy, although it is difficult to determine the exact extent. What is certain is that until the end of the seventeenth century, Greek pirates acted as local bandits of limited range, mainly on islands that offered refuge. According to testimonies, Mykonos was one such island. Pirates usually formed excellent relations with local merchants, who bought their loot at low prices; with local authorities, who left them undisturbed; and even with foreign merchants.

On Mykonos in particular, piracy was so widespread that it took on the character of a “communal enterprise.” The special relationship between the islanders and both local and foreign pirates was a basic factor of prosperity. Thus pirates were not clearly distinct from captains, and the first “urban” elements of the Aegean came out of commercial development and the accumulation of capital, which was partly based on piracy.

According to one documented version, in the Venetian Quarter of Mykonos, on the southern side of the Kastro, the houses of the captains had their famous sea-facing doors: semi-urban doors at the waterfront that led directly, or through a courtyard, to underground storage areas. These were used for the secret transfer and hiding of pirate loot.

The main agricultural products were grapes, especially for wine, and barley. Secondary products included other cereals, figs, a few garden products, dairy and livestock products, fishing, and hunting. There may also once have been olive trees. At the beginning of the eighteenth century, Mykonos produced around 25,000 to 30,000 barrels of wine per year.

Natural and social factors limited agricultural production: lack of water, absence of irrigation works, soil composition, the sloped surface of the land, and the island’s position on international routes. These conditions pushed the Mykonians early toward maritime occupations. From the beginning of the sixteenth century, Greeks turned toward the sea. Some served as sailors in the Ottoman fleet; others sought work in naval expeditions; others enlisted as armed seamen; and others acted as pirates, either independently or in cooperation with buccaneers, often under the flag of a foreign power.

Commercial shipping and prosperity

The peak of Mykonian commercial shipping began at the start of the eighteenth century and was based on the fact that the route of French shipping toward Smyrna and Constantinople passed between Tinos and Mykonos, while the English route passed between Andros and Euboea. Thus, at the beginning of the century, Mykonos possessed forty to fifty large caïques used in short-distance maritime trade, especially for island trade with the Peloponnese and Asia Minor. On this route alone there were more than one hundred boats and a total population of about 3,000 inhabitants.

At the end of the eighteenth century, within the context of the Napoleonic blockade and the English counter-blockade, the merchant captains of the Aegean grew rich mainly through the illicit provisioning of blockaded ports with grain. This was also a period of great prosperity for Mykonos, lasting approximately from 1750 to 1815. During this period, the newly formed bourgeois class gave Chora the form of the town that it has largely preserved until today.

At that time most of the shipowners’ houses were built, often with multiple façades or forms, both in the older neighborhoods of the Kastro and Parikia, through reconstruction or additions, and especially in the newer neighborhoods of Alexandra, Matogianni, and Agia Kyriaki. It was also the period when many of the larger churches of Chora were built.

At the beginning of the nineteenth century, the population of the island had already reached 4,000 inhabitants. Its maritime development was combined with, or complemented by, the development of manufacturing with an export orientation in two sectors: the weaving of cotton, woolen, and silk fabrics, mainly carried out by the women of the island, and the milling of flour and baking of bread and ship’s biscuit. The numerical predominance of women was evident because the men were often absent on maritime expeditions, and this impressed travelers. The development of the second sector, flour milling and bread-making, is a particularly interesting example of the ecological character of the island’s economic history.

Revolution, decline, and tourism

Later, the island’s merchant fleet entered the service of the Greek Revolution. The population immediately before and after the Revolution increased because of refugees arriving from other islands, mainly Chios, Psara, Kasos, and Crete. From the middle of the nineteenth century, emigration abroad began, as did the decline of shipping, with the dominance of steam power and the inability of the captains to cooperate in order to meet the new, increased need for capital.

The period of occupation during the Second World War finally exhausted the Mykonians through hunger and depopulation. Only in the 1950s, with the development of tourism, which began with the growing prosperity of the inhabitants, did a new period of economic prosperity open for the island.

Thus the sun, the air, the sea, and Delos, which for eighteen centuries the Mykonians had used in order to sell its marble, cultivate its land, and graze their goats and sheep, entered the economic circuit once again. In a new form, Mykonos survived and developed by exploiting its least costly natural resources.

Settlement structure

Mykonos: one town and hamlets.

It appears that, at least from the medieval period onward, Chora was always the only settlement of Mykonos. The rest of the island was inhabited only by hamlets, that is, denser zones of permanent rural settlement. These were scattered both in the eastern part of the island, on the eastern slopes of the central mountainous mass, forming the core of the community of Ano Mera and Kalo Livadi, and in the western part of the island.

The mills

The Upper Mills served the needs of the rural population, while the roughly ten Lower Mills processed grain brought from other regions and unloaded by ships at the nearby anchorage of Vido. The mills either reloaded the flour, or the flour was made into bread or ship’s biscuit in the many large ovens of Chora. These supplied the foreign ships that stopped at the island specifically to acquire provisions. After the full bread-making cycle was completed, the two almost cost-free local sources of energy used in pre-industrial processing were water and wind, the wind and ovens of a poor, barren land.

Even more noteworthy, and characteristic of the way Mykonos’s economic and social development functioned, is the fact that mills, boats, and fishing vessels were often organized in cooperative forms. Shares in these cooperative structures were arranged according to technical and economic participation.

Chora: harbours and setting

Travelers mention more than one secondary settlement on Mykonos. Others wrote of a second village, but in this case it should probably be understood as Palaiokastro, the settlement east of the bay of Panormos with the medieval mansion of the Ghizi family.

Chora lies on the western coast of the island, which is also the most interesting part in terms of bays and coves. It is located in the middle of a roughly large and deep gulf, formed by the bays of Agios Stefanos and Tourlos, or Agios Georgios of Tourlos, to the north, the isthmus between Korfos and Ornos to the south, the peninsula of Agios Ioannis or Diakoftis, and the small island to the west.

Of these bays, Tourlos offered the best anchorage for large ships, protected from the northern winds. Korfos also had a particularly safe anchorage for large ships. The smaller cove of Kanalia, on the Diakoftis peninsula, was used mainly for loading grain during the period of prosperity of sailing navigation and of milling and bread-making activity. The bay of Ornos also had an anchorage, but it was exposed to southerly winds, which, although rarer than the northerlies, are nevertheless stronger.

Chora itself had a small and unsafe harbor, formed between the coast east of the Kastro and the islets of Agios Nikolakis of Kadenas or Gialos. Since this position was exposed to the northern winds, the harbor of Chora was used only for loading and only in favorable weather. With the north winds, boats fled to the bay of Tourlos. Thus, the lack of a safe harbor for Chora was compensated for by its central position between the outer bays of Tourlos and Korfos.

It is necessary to define geographically the extent of the traditional settlement of Chora of Mykonos, because its present condition gives, at first glance, the impression of a settlement five times larger. The traditional settlement of Chora, at the beginning of the 1960s, was still separated from the island’s rural hamlets by agricultural land, especially by the slopes of Vouno to the east. These slopes formed a rim at about 100 metres above sea level and constituted the settlement’s immediate natural frame.

The settlement is built on a level area that forms two projections, and at the same time two slight rises toward the sea: the hillock of the Kastro further north, which creates the hollow of the harbor, and the rise with the Lower Mills further south. The level ground on which the town was built, with the exception of Pano Matogianni, gives Chora of Mykonos a form completely different from most Aegean settlements. Those settlements, spread amphitheatrically on slopes, are perceived more as sculptural volumes than as spaces. By contrast, Chora of Mykonos is perceived as a labyrinthine inner urban space of life, cut through a compact white mass of unified character.

A white, labyrinthine town

This impression is strongly helped by the unified style of the buildings, the overwhelming majority of which are two-storeyed. It is also helped by the presence of balconies, stairs, terraces, steps, and by the care taken in maintaining the street surfaces, especially the outer façades of the buildings.

The feeling that one is inside an interior space, with the only reminders of the exterior and natural world being the view of the sea at the end of an alley, or the sound of the wind, creates a strong sense of intimacy and protection. This feeling is only gradually lifted when the passer-by arrives at Paralia or Alefkandra.

The urban form of Chora is a remarkable example, though not an exceptional one, of balance between the whole and its parts. On the one hand, there are elements that give the urban ensemble its harmonious continuity. On the other hand, the individual elements and parts preserve a certain individuality, which stands out through its variety.

This aesthetic observation can be established on two levels. First, at the level of the overall image of the typical house of Chora, which is characterized by austere abstract coherence, even though it is the result of the gradual addition of sections of different forms. This development may have unfolded over centuries. Second, the same is true at the level of the typical public living space of the settlement: the street. In one glance, one understands how labyrinthine the urban whole is, while at the same time perceiving the variety of the house façades that shape its front.

It is therefore worth analyzing these two points in more detail:

  • the urban evolution of Chora;
  • the form and function of the typical house of Chora of Mykonos.

The urban fabric and evolution of Chora

The gradual evolution of Chora through time is clearly reflected in the form of its urban fabric. Until the middle of the seventeenth century, Chora was probably limited to the Kastro, a rise in the land at the northwestern edge of present-day Chora.

The Kastro and the first medieval settlement

The Kastro stood about 10 metres from the sea. It was one of the island’s two fortified settlements. Technically speaking, it was a weak form of fortification: it was formed by the strengthened outer walls of the surrounding houses, rather than by a true fortress-castle.

Similar fortifications were used, between the fourteenth century and the first years of Ottoman rule, as places of protection for the inhabitants of island settlements in the Aegean against raids. Effective protection, however, was offered only by the true fortress-type fortifications of the larger islands, such as Tinos. Smaller and less important islands, such as Mykonos, had rudimentary enclosing walls that could not withstand powerful attacks.

Another fortified place is also mentioned on Mykonos: Palaiokastro, in the interior of the island, on a height dominating the bay of Panormos to the north, where the ruins of the local palace of the Ghizi family are found. The Kastro of Chora nevertheless had basic equipment for the protection and feeding of the inhabitants enclosed within it in the event of a raid. Evidence of this is the Kastrapnos Mill, traces of which survive at the northwestern edge, almost directly above the sea.

The urban layout of this first medieval settlement of Mykonos has not survived. Its present structure clearly resulted from the construction of new buildings over older ones, which were either destroyed or gradually demolished as the settlement expanded beyond the walls. This expansion must have begun in the early or middle eighteenth century, perhaps between 1754 and 1782.

The present street layout of the Kastro, with its comfortable open spaces around Paraportiani, does not even approximate the urban image of the medieval settlement, which, within a total area of 7.2 stremmas, housed at least 2,000 permanent inhabitants. Such density could only have been achieved under extremely poor urban conditions. We must therefore assume that uncovered spaces, mainly streets, occupied only 10% of the total area, and that 60% of those were katastegia, that is, “long, narrow, shared covered corridors beneath dwellings and sometimes churches.” We must also assume that churches were built on 10% of the plots, that plot coverage was complete, and that most buildings were three storeys high, with an average building coefficient of 2.8. Finally, the inhabitants of the Kastro lived in about 500 rooms, with an average occupancy of four people per room.

The living conditions inside the Kastro are described vividly by V. Kyriazopoulos:

Before the town spread outside the Kastro, and for many years afterward, the small enclosure of the Kastro described above was suffocatingly full of buildings. The houses there did not have the form of distinct buildings, but formed a confused system of cells, small spaces placed next to each other in complete disorder and at different levels, so that the description “the Kastro was like a pomegranate” is very apt.

If we consider that in this cramped space of 7,200 square metres, 2,000 or more people lived permanently, together with many animals, perhaps pigs and hens; that the dead were buried in family chapels beside the houses; if we also think of the abundance of rats and vermin of every kind; that waste was thrown into the narrow lanes, where roaming pigs cleaned it up; that water was scarce in summer; and that no real sewerage system or medical care existed, we can imagine the stench of this place and understand the spread of tuberculosis and the occasional great epidemics.

In fact, the hygienic and living conditions there must have been terrible, though not worse than those in similar castles on other islands. The antidotes to all this were the winter rain, the frequent whitewashing of everything, and the north winds. Especially in summer, life would have been unlivable without the natural factor of the annual seasonal wind, the meltemi. These violent, cool, clean masses of air entered everywhere, renewed the air, lowered the temperature, and carried everything away.

Expansion of Chora beyond the Kastro

The growth of the population and the improvement of the island’s economy, through the flourishing of commercial shipping and crafts during the peaceful years before the outbreak of the Revolution, led to the gradual expansion of the settlement of Chora over an area many times larger than that of the Kastro.

We distinguish Mbarkia or Parkia and Parikia, the area of the first eighteenth-century expansion. It forms the central nucleus of the newer settlement, covering about 20 stremmas, with dense construction and a labyrinthine, initially unclear street network.

We can also distinguish the newer neighborhoods of Matogiannia, with an orthogonal street grid, and Niochori, from the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, with linear development. Finally, there is the central area of gardens, Limni, which was gradually enclosed by residential zones.

Among these areas, some are characterized by urban regularity, while others seem to fill in the gaps of the earlier ones, and their urban form remains unclear and confused. Yet a more careful look at the network of these later small streets, combined with information from older inhabitants and with observation of the established routes linking the settlement’s core to the rural roads, removes this ambiguity and reveals the hierarchical order of the street network.

It also reveals two alternative ways in which the “building blocks” were formed: the planned or urban type, and the accidental or rural type. The first type has a regularity that is not geometric, but functional; in other words, it expresses a clear compositional intention. Usually its form is narrow and elongated, because it consists either of one row of through-buildings or of a double row.

These blocks may preserve a memory of older fortified settlements, where the uninterrupted line of façades formed the wall, as C. Papas observes. The second type has shapes that, as “building blocks,” seem completely unjustified; but they make sense if they are understood as former agricultural plots that were later subdivided for building. It is easy, then, to imagine how a dry-stone wall that once followed the turns and slight changes in level of a rural path, marking the boundary of a farmstead, was later replaced by a polygonal row of residential façades.

In the end, however, all these structural differences in Chora’s urban fabric, from one area to another, fade and harmonize into a whole that we perceive as unified. Despite the gradual evolution of the settlement and its heterogeneous street layout, its urban form has a unified character. This is mainly due to its chromatic and volumetric cohesion: its completely white, compact, mostly two-storeyed mass, and the homogeneity of the architectural elements of its buildings.

The street and the house

The public spaces of the settlement are usually small. One rarely encounters a square. The typical shared space is the street. At the scale of the street, one observes a remarkable balance between the whole and its parts. The whole is the street; the parts are the exterior façades of the houses that define it.

The characteristic street is formed by rows of houses, the great majority of which belong to two types: narrow-fronted and wide-fronted houses. The houses of the same block or cluster usually belong to the same type and share remarkably similar characteristics.

The façades of a row of narrow-fronted houses, for example, show great similarity in their dimensions and an identical organization of the façade. They are two-storeyed, with a ground-floor height of about 2.50 metres, an upper-floor height of 3 to 5 metres, and a frontage usually of 4 to 5 metres. Solid surfaces dominate over openings. There are two openings per floor, door and window, with a central transom for lighting and ventilating the high-ceilinged upper room, the moutsoulos. The upper floor has separate access from the street through an external stone stair leaning against the outer wall. This occurs whether the house is used as a single dwelling or whether the ground floor and upper floor belong to different owners.

The morphology of the façade elements is also common: the extremely simple wooden balusters on the stairs, the wooden bountia or balconies, with equally restrained wooden railings, the treatment of the openings, the channels or drainpipes, and the chimneys. Similar morphology, similar organization, and similar dimensions on the façades produce an urban image in which individualized elements are balanced with a unified whole.

Where individual elements, the houses, contribute, through their external morphological kinship, to the creation of a unified and harmonious whole, the street, they nevertheless preserve their individuality through quantitative and geometric differences.

At another level, the volumetric compactness of Mykonian houses and the openness of Chora’s urban fabric were once less clear. The streets were bridged, at rather high density, by built volumes. The katastegia, which extended the surface of neighboring houses over shared spaces and created covered streets, gave Chora the form of a complex honeycomb, with frequent alternations and contrasts of solid and void, shade and light, private and shared spaces. The only signs that remain today from many such covered passages are the bases of the arches that covered the street and supported the external walls, or even the floors, of rooms above.

It is more reasonable to suppose that the same conditions of spatial narrowness produced similar solutions in the house to those that shaped the ship’s stair in naval architecture, even though the analogies, the steepness, and the elements of the interior stair recall the ship’s ladder. Even if there is some doubt about the interior stairs, in the exterior stairs the available length of the façade and the position of the entrance door of the neighboring house certainly impose a steep slope. Moreover, the narrowness of the street does not allow another kind of stair, except a straight, single-flight one. The only variation, for an easier ascent, is the addition of two or three steps where the stair meets the street, set crosswise to its direction.

The organization and construction of the Mykonian house

The interior organization of the narrow-fronted house into spaces of distinct use is rudimentary. The elongated space is subdivided into two parts, one toward the street and one toward the opposite side. On both the ground floor and the upper floor, this division is usually made with a light partition wall, a bagdati or wooden partition, especially on the upper floor.

This is because, in the narrow-fronted house, the support of the floor and roof is carried by beams placed parallel to the façade wall, without any transverse load-bearing element. Only in the case of a large house depth, and in order to tie together the two party walls, does the dividing wall become a structural element and is built in masonry.

The front part of the house is larger in area and multifunctional. It served as the sala, as a sitting room, often as a work space for women, with a loom, and even as a kitchen. The rear part was mainly used for sleeping, though sometimes also for cooking. Because the older building blocks with narrow-fronted houses are more numerous, and because, under the conditions in which it developed, space was a basic necessity, many narrow-fronted houses have party walls on three sides and are lit only from the façade wall.

In this case, the ground floor, whose height was clearly lower than that of the upper floor, 2.50 metres as opposed to 3, 4, or 5 metres, often remains single-roomed. This was done either to avoid making lighting and ventilation conditions even worse, or because it was used as storage and as temporary lodging for rural people, inhabitants of the villages and the countryside.

The overwhelming majority of narrow-fronted houses have two openings per floor: one door and one window. The doors of the ground floors open directly onto the street, and in the newer, more spacious neighborhoods a small courtyard may intervene, while the doors of the upper floors open onto the wooden bountia.

The constant presence of the external stone stair, and more rarely a wooden one, as well as the possibility of internal communication through a wooden “ship’s ladder,” always steeply inclined, whose opening closes with a trapdoor, allows us to suppose that the house could function either as a single whole or as two separate apartments: one ground-floor apartment and one upper-floor apartment.

In the wide-fronted houses, the organization shows somewhat greater variety, but its basic elements remain the same. The plan is divided into two unequal parts, of which the larger is always the sala.

The wide-fronted house and interior organization

The remaining part is usually subdivided into two very small bedrooms, with access from the main space. The arrangement of the façade of the wide-fronted type is more free, and is not governed by the order seen in the narrow-fronted type.

In the wide-fronted houses, the doors are placed vertically on the façade, while in the direction of the large opening, parallel to the façade, one or two, depending on the depth of the house, travedzenia, or main beams, are placed approximately in the middle of the house. These subdivide the plan into smaller rectangular spaces. On these travedzenia, or on walls that play the same structural role, rest the ribes, the main horizontal load-bearing structure of the roof.

Older sanitary spaces were not integrated into dwellings. On some façades, a section of the wooden balcony was closed in at some point and used as a rudimentary sanitary space, something that generally seems to have happened in more recent years. Elsewhere, the sanitary space was built beneath the stone stair, in the offered space between the private and the shared area. It was often enclosed by a low wall, so that a small courtyard was created.

Most houses in Chora are two-storeyed. Some, especially captains’ houses, such as those in the northern commercial sector of Venetia and in Alefkandra, are three-storeyed. There are also many houses with a lower second storey above the upper floor or the ground floor, occupying only one third or one half of the plan, and always the rear part. In this case, the floor level of the second storey is one or two steps lower than the level of the roof terrace.

Fireplaces for heating do not appear in Mykonos. They are usually built in kitchens and are used exclusively for cooking, which is why they are called mageiria. Often, for reasons of discretion and cleanliness, the fireplaces are closed with wooden leaves like cupboards, especially in captains’ houses, although they can also be found with many other functions.

To save interior space, because of the small dimensions of the houses and the great thickness of the stone walls, the smoke flues of the fireplaces projected from the surface of the exterior wall. The entire projection rested on four or five wooden brackets, which form a characteristic element of the façades.

The construction of the Mykonian house follows the general principles and shows the usual characteristics of Cycladic building, shaped by the particularities of the island’s economy and resources.

Construction materials, roofs, masonry, and plaster

In the construction of the roof terrace, the main beams were made of imported wood, oak, cypress, plane tree, chestnut, or larch, as were the secondary beams, made of chestnut or elm hardwood. Above them were placed boards or reeds, in a layer about 3 to 4 cm thick. Then came a layer of seaweed, dried after being washed and cleaned. On top of this was placed soil, about 10 to 12 cm thick, and finally a layer of mortar, lime and coarse sand, sometimes also kourasani. When kourasani was not used, the waterproof quality of the final layer was perhaps due to the type of lime or to another admixture material. The roof boards were made of good wood, and when their length reached 1.00 to 1.30 m, they were also used as beams.

The masonry in Chora is rubble masonry and often slab masonry. Marble is frequently used for small frames, mainly around doors, windows, and openings; for corner stones; for surfaces around openings where a row of dressed stones was sometimes inserted; and for columns, supports, or friezes, especially in newer houses after the end of the nineteenth century, when certain neoclassical elements began to appear in Mykonian architecture. Marble is Parian and, more rarely, Naxiot. Moreover, many of the marbles found in Chora clearly come from Delos.

Mykonian plasters are especially plastic in form. They are pressed plasters made of coarse sand and lime. The sand used for roofs is about the size of a lentil, while for walls it is about half that size. In the past, plaster was applied by hand, without a straightedge, and was not smoothed down in the modern way; instead, it was pressed. For roof terraces, a special trowel was used. Essential tools of the mason also included the pelekia, which held the mortar, and the dintili, or plumb line.

The arrangement of openings, lintels, frames, parapets, and the evolution of the two types of openings in Cycladic architecture have been analyzed extensively by C. Pappas. The basic types of openings still survive and are reproduced today in Mykonos. The type that tends to disappear completely is the old door-window type. The type that now tends to dominate is the window with three or four leaves, and the door with two leaves and shutters, allowing the upper part to open independently from the lower part, and the lower part independently from the upper.

On the façades of houses, characteristic elements include the gutters, which were either formed as concave channels in the plaster or built out of ceramic pipe sections. On the roof terrace, the formation of the parapet is especially characteristic. It retains rainwater.

The countryside and its hamlets

The landscape of the Mykonian countryside is austere and extremely spare in aesthetic expression. White and ochre dominate, together with smooth or rough, often whitewashed rocks, usually joined by dry-stone walls. On them, or beside them, are planted white farmhouse dwellings, gathered in small rural settlements, or scattered farm-chapels and churches.

Trees are rare; they appear in certain sheltered ravines, in only a few valleys. The dry-stone walls, austere and skillful arrangements made of the black natural stone of the island, define enclosures, vineyards, and grazing fields, and accompany the rural roads, the gates, and the smaller passages. The footpath network becomes denser around the island’s heights and projections. A second concentration of the road network appears around the settlement of Ano Mera.

Rural architecture and the microcosm of the hamlet

In strong contrast with the brown and grey colours of the fields and rocks stand the white hamlets and farmhouses. These consist of a multitude of small, clearly distinguishable building volumes. They are elongated spaces with specialized uses and functions, forming the strict microcosm of rural life.

One can distinguish: the house, usually two or three rooms in a row, with a small open or partly roofed courtyard; the barn and the cellar; the oven; the stable or animal shelter; the pigeon house; the place for keeping animals and producing manure; the winepress; the trough for watering animals; sometimes the threshing floor and the courtyard; and sometimes the church, with a red-tiled roof. The church is sometimes integrated into the hamlet as a family ossuary, and more rarely appears together with a windmill, especially in rural hamlets where care and use were shared by two or three families.

The hamlets of Mykonos often touch, or are founded directly upon, the same granite rocks that define the character of the island’s landscape. Like elsewhere in the Cyclades, they are extremely intelligent responses to the needs of a people who had very limited resources.

Their organization is standardized: single-storey houses arranged in sequence, often built in different periods, one beside another. They either form an elongated volume, with each façade having a separate entrance, or they are arranged in the shape of a Γ or Π, enclosing a small paved courtyard on two or three sides.

Courtyards, orientation, and the ecological logic of the hamlet

In the first case, the outer façade, toward the side of the road or the neighbouring property, is closed and completely without openings. In the case of the women’s courtyard, its orientation is south-southeast or southwest. Often, in the courtyard, instead of slab paving, the local polished granite is used as flooring.

With the living spaces of human beings, a small outdoor space is created. Often, one or two projecting steps define the courtyard or the passage that connects the spaces of the house, separating it from the public space or from the surrounding circulation area of animals.

Inside the house, the building elements are the same as in the urban house of Chora: the same general arrangement of the mageirio or cooking area, the same ledges and shelves for storing food and utensils, and the volto, the shelf above the wooden window, for storing bread. Even the construction methods, techniques, and other elements are the same or similar. The geometry, however, is freer; the detailing is more raw; the equipment is more rudimentary. In other words, the economy of means is even more sharply expressed.

These characteristics emphasize the primary element of the architecture of the Mykonian hamlets. More than anything else, this is what makes one see it as the same kind of “natural” and unrefined ecological necessity as the dry-stone walls, the terraces, the rocks, and the cactus hedges of the island.

The Mykonian hamlet is the highest and most refined functional creation of a people who chose, on a rocky island, with only the sun, the air, the sea, and the inventiveness of poverty as resources.
